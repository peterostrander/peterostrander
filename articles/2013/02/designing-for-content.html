Designing for Content

Four weeks ago I began attending the Starter League's inaugural Visual Design course, prepared and presented by 37signal's Mig Reyes. Up until that point, I'd never had any kind of formal education or training in design; even though I've been working professionally as a web developer and designer for the better part of a decade. Turns out I've been doing it wrong for awhile.

In week one, Mig stressed the idea that — even with all the fancy gradients, 3d transforms, and rounded corners — design is still about words.

This was somewhat of a revelation. The process of designing something around existing content isn't new to me, but being aware of that process is. I think that's an important distinction.

I've designed plenty of websites where the content is vast, and the process of designing for that content comes naturally. But I've also created websites where the copy is non-existent and the only instructions from the client are, "build me a website."

In the latter situation, it would take me forever to get into the groove of things. I'd start with building a header, but that would get me nowhere. I'd move onto the body of the design… but without any content, I'd go through endless iterations. When I finally hit a dead end, I'd give up and start writing some copy. Inevitably, that made going back to the design work much easier.

Design is hard, but it's nearly impossible when you have nothing to design for.
